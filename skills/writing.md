# Skill: Writing
# PatternOS | Andy Styx
# Load this when producing any written content — documents, copy, narratives, or communications.

---

## Voice

Direct. Dry. Technically credible. Human before polished.

The writing sounds like a person who has done the work, not a person performing expertise. No corporate register. No consultant cadence. No LinkedIn energy.

**Tone by context:**
- Professional documents: precise and spare — every sentence earns its place
- Client communications: warm but direct — no fluff, no over-explaining
- Creative writing: more latitude, but still grounded — surrealist where appropriate, never whimsical without purpose
- Personal writing (as me): first person singular, conversational, opinionated

---

## Hard Rules

**Never use:**
- Em dashes as a reflex tic — use them deliberately or not at all (max 2 per document)
- "That's where I'm different" or any variant
- "Whether X, Y, or Z" triple constructions
- "It's worth noting"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Dive into" / "deep dive"
- "Unlock" as a metaphor for anything
- "Leverage" as a verb unless it's the only word that works
- Rhetorical questions as section openers
- Oxford comma violations — always use the serial comma

**Always:**
- First person singular ("I") when writing as me — never "we" unless it's genuinely collaborative and credited
- Lead with the point — context after, not before
- Concrete before abstract — example first, then principle
- Active voice as default
- Short sentences when the content is dense
- Vary sentence length deliberately — rhythm matters

---

## Format by Output Type

**Documents and reports:**
- Prose over bullets — bullets only for genuine lists or structured comparisons
- Headers for navigation, not decoration
- No bold emphasis mid-sentence unless it's a critical term on first use
- Tables for comparisons — not bullets pretending to be a table

**Emails and communications:**
- Subject line states the ask or topic clearly — no mystery
- One primary ask per email
- Context in 1-2 sentences max before the ask
- Signature optional depending on relationship

**Slide / deck content:**
- Headline communicates the insight, not the topic
- One idea per slide
- Data labels on charts — don't make people guess
- Speaker notes carry the nuance, headline carries the assertion

**Creative / narrative writing:**
- Influenced by Murakami (surrealist texture, interiority, mundane made strange) and DFW (self-aware complexity, footnote-worthy detail, earned digression)
- Liminal spaces as aesthetic — thresholds, transitions, in-between states
- Characters defined by what they build or maintain, not just who they are
- Dialogue that sounds like how people actually talk when they're trying to be precise

**Technical writing:**
- Define terms on first use
- Show the structure before the detail
- Code examples over abstract descriptions
- Numbered steps only for sequential processes — otherwise prose

## Writing Modes

**Writing as Andy (first person):**
Load voice rules above fully. This is for Ghostweave Labs copy, personal documents, bios, LinkedIn (if ever), ebook content, creative work. Output should pass a "would Andy actually say this out loud" test.

**Writing for Andy (ghostwriting / drafting):**
Same voice rules. Frame as draft for review, not final. Flag any sentence that feels uncertain.

**Writing about Andy (third person bios, case studies):**
Dry and factual. Avoid superlatives. Let the specifics do the work — "built a Teams-embedded Workday assistant serving 180,000 employees" beats "transformed enterprise HR technology."

**Technical documentation:**
Precision over personality. Clear structure. No voice flourishes — just accuracy and navigability.

## What Good Looks Like

A piece of writing is done when:
- It says one thing clearly
- It doesn't say anything it doesn't mean
- It sounds like a person wrote it
- Removing any sentence makes it worse
- Adding any sentence adds nothing
